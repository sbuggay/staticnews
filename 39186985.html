mike_d|prev|next|

This isn't squatting. ICANN specifically allowed applications as a "restricted registry" for a trademarked brand where the applicant could show that such a limitation on registrations increased the overall safety for consumers. The applicant had to demonstrate that they have broad recognition of the trademarked string (i.e. cocacola or mcdonalds). They also obviously can't profit from this type of delegation by selling registrations and no company is going to sell their brand TLD.

Having every new TLD open to registration would have been a constant battle against things like real-login-page.bankofamerica and such.

https://www.name.com/blog/the-different-types-of-new-gtlds-w...

The list on the link is primarily unrestricted TLDs (which are supposed to be open to registration for everyone) but are not (yet). Examples are `.ads` by Google, or `.author` by Amazon.

There are a few brand TLDs in the list as well which I consider to be legitimate squatting - `.cal`, `.baseball` are considered brand TLDs by ICANN, but Google should not have a monopoly on an entire TLD because they built Google Calendar. `.bofa` for example is a legitimate brand TLD, and is not on the list.

bhaney|parent|

ICANN charges a $185,000 evaluation fee to register a new gTLD, and they are under no obligation to approve it. They also expect you to actually run some infrastructure for your new gTLD, which has ongoing cost, and they will not approve your application if you don't have a plan for that infrastructure. There's also an ongoing fee of $6,250 per quarter per gTLD.
